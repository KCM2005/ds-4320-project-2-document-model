# Headline
Targeting within 30-day hospital readmissions: Top 10 key diabetes-related primary diagnoses by gender unveiled

# Hook
Diabetes gender divide: See how within 30-day hospital readmission rates for top 10 primary diagnoses differ

# Problem Statement
Diabetic patients in the US face a significant challenge with hospital readmissions, with nearly 1 in 5 patients (20%) readmitted within 30 days (Rubin et al, 2023). These readmissions are often due to complications from underlying diabetes-related primary diagnoses (comorbidities) such as heart disease, kidney disease, and neuropathy (Bodke et al, 2023). Comorbidities complicate treatment, leading to poorer outcomes and increased hospital readmissions. However, current clinical practices frequently overlook stratification of primary diagnoses by gender; yet research shows that diabetic women patients have a 27 % higher relative risk of stroke and a 44 % higher relative risk of coronary heart disease compared to men (Peters et al, 2015). This stratification oversight can lead to inadequate treatment plans, as comorbidities manifest and impact diabetes outcomes differently in men and women. As such, my specific focus on stratifying diabetes-related primary comorbidities by gender could enable personalized treatment approaches, improve outcomes and reduce gender-based health disparities.

# Solution Description
This study analyzed 30-day readmission risk among diabetic patients by focusing on diabetes-related primary diagnoses using the dataset consisting of 101,766 diabetic patients in 130 US hospitals, and analyzed the patients by gender ((https://archive.ics.uci.edu/dataset/296/diabetes+130-us+hospitals+for+years+1999-2008). The findings revealed top 10 primary diagnoses driving hospital readmissions for both female and male diabetic patients, as visualized in Figure 1. Notable findings are that:

- Stroke and Heart failure: These are the most common primary comorbidities driving a within 30-day hospital readmission rates among diabetic patients for both genders, although females have a slightly higher risk rate than males for both stroke (16.4% and 16.1%) and heart failure (14.3% and 13.9%), respectively.

- Chronic bronchitis and pneumonia: These primary diagnoses are more prevalent among males than females, with chronic bronchitis having a rate of ~14% male and ~ 12% females, while pneumonia has a readmission rate of ~9.6% males and ~ 8.4% females ).

- Respiratory symptoms: This is the only primary diagnosis that has almost equal readmission rate for both female (7.2%) and male (7.3%) patients

- For the rest of the primary diagnoses: Females have a slightly higher readmission rate compared to males. For example, heart attack (10.6% females and 10.1% males were readmitted), cardiac dysrhythmias (10.1% females and 8.1% males were readmitted), and osteoarthritis (11.2% females and 8.1% males were readmitted))
